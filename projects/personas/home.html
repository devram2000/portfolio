Television Remote Interface

Do you think your television remote is designed to be the best interface for your television?

The television remote is included as an accessory for a television to be a handheld and distant point of control. As evident in the above sketch of an example remote design, there are many different sections on the remote, each with many buttons for various television functionality. However, all television remotes do not follow the same structure and semantics for their button setup.

The question then becomes: does the multitude of buttons and varying structure of television remote interfaces affect user experience?

To gain some insight on this question, I observed casual users interacting with television remotes and then interviewed them on their expectations and experiences from a television remote.

Observations

Question: Do you mind if I ask you to do some tasks to watch how you operate your television remote(s)?

These tasks included switching to a news channel, searching for a random channel, changing volume, switching back and forth between channels, and changing the video source.

Interviewed User 1:

This user was confident and quick about pressing all buttons for each of the tasks asked. Even the more advanced features on the remote (like the search, last channel, and video source buttons) were utilized with ease. The user appeared annoyed when having to type using the arrow pad when searching for a channel.

Interviewed User 2:

The user was able to do the majority of the tasks, but they did not use the more advanced features of the remote to achieve the tasks (ie. scrolled through the guide to look for the channel instead of searching and scrolled to switch back instead of using last). The user pressed semi-random buttons to try to change the video source (they said they did it before) but gave up eventually. Also, they pressed the return button multiple times when the television was loading even though the signal was received.

Interviewed User 3:

This user was not particularly hesitant about pressing the buttons but was slow and decisive. The user had the channel number memorized for a news station and used the number pad to type it in. They used most of the advanced features except for the search feature; the user stated that they knew the task could be completed with search, but it would be too much of a hassle for them.

Questions

Take a look at a sample of the questions asked to users after the observations! Consider asking yourself about your expectations and experiences from a television remote while looking at these:
  1. How would you define a television remote?
  2. Why would you want to use a television remote?
  3. What do you expect the physical composition of a television remote in terms of size and accessibility to be like?
  4. Can you describe a situation where a television remote interface caused you issues or was an inconvenience?
  5. Picture a situation where you have to use multiple remote controls at once to operate different devices (like one for the cable box and one for the television). On a scale from 1 to 5 with 1 being no inconvenience and 5 being an extreme inconvenience, how much of an inconvenience would the process of having to use multiple remotes for one system be?
  6. What features do you expect a television remote to provide?
  7. Would you prefer more or less button features on a television remote and why?

Response Summaries

Interviewed User 1:

This user described themself as technologically savvy, and they use their television remote often. The user was very adamant about the television remote needing to transition towards having less buttons and repeatedly suggested having a smartphone-like touch screen interface. They stated that television remotes are unnecessarily complicated and not user friendly: too many buttons that do not have intuitive purposes. They feel very inconvenienced whenever dealing with a remote interface problem.

Interviewed User 2:

This user described themself as not being too experienced with technology, but they use their television remote every day. The user stated that the interfaces of their two remotes (for cable and television) are inconvenient and very different, but they have gotten used to them over time. They try to figure things out on the remotes either by asking someone or pressing random buttons that seem related. The user does not know what "more than half" of the buttons do.

Interviewed User 3:

This user described themself as technologically proficient, but they do not use their television remote too often. The user wanted the remote to be as ergonomic and tactile as possible. Their expectations for a television remote are low because they were raised without one (they needed to go to the television to control it). They want the remote to have a better visual feedback loop of signals (specifically when trying to change the video source in cable mode on a universal remote instead of on television mode).

Personas

Using the trends observed across interviewed users, I created two personas with empathy maps to understand and empathize with television remote users.

Persona 1: Doubtful Diane

Doubtful Diane is a retired grandmother who watches television every day, but only typically watches two shows (the news and cooking channels).

Today, she has guests who want to watch something else.

Thinks
I hope they don't want me to do anything fancy with the remote.
Why does there have to be so many buttons?
I wish someone else could do this for me.
Was it this button to pull up the remote guide?
Feels
Hesitant to use the remote because of its complexity.
Content with watching a show she doesn't like if it's easier.
Willing to use the remote instead of getting up to change the channel.
Says
"Let me keep trying to figure this out for you."
"Why don't we just watch this show?"
"My grandson is a genius because he knows how to operate this thing."
"Oops, I scrolled past the channel! I have to start over."
Does
Scrolls through the whole guide to find new channels.
Memorizes favorite channel numbers.
Presses random buttons when unsure how to do something.

Persona 2: Irritable Igor

Irritable Igor is a middle-aged bachelor who works as a traveling salesman.

His favorite show starts in 5 minutes on television, so he is trying to find the channel from the television in his current hotel.

Thinks
Why is this remote so different from mine?
I wish I had just recorded the show from my house.
Is the remote signal going through?
Feels
Stressed out from his job.
Annoyed at needing to use the remote arrow pad to type.
Upset at the possibility of missing the first part of his show.
Frustrated that the signal is not showing feedback on the remote.
Says
"Elmo's World... ELMO'S WORLD... Why can't I talk to the remote?"
"I want to sue whoever designed this remote."
"I can't believe this is taking so long."
Does
Uses the search functionality instead of scrolling through the guide.
Keeps on pressing the same button on the remote even though the signal was received.
Smashes the remote on the ground.
Calls the remote company and leaves a scathing complaint.

Conclusions

An experience with the television remote like the one shown in the storyboard did not ruin the night of Diane and her family, but it was a non-trivial inconvenience. However, isn't the whole point of the remote to be an interface for the television that makes life easier?

If you think the television remote isn't designed to be the best interface for a television, I recommend you to be the change you want to see in the world and design a better remote!
